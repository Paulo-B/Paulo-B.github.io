Control is a game developed by Remedy Entertainment, a Finnish studio. The creators present a unique atmosphere that should be praised in terms of creativity, going away from the typical fantasy, mythology, or on the other end, the sci-fi that we are so used to. For that, they venture into the realm of the paranormal, but not the folklore kind. Instead, one set inside a brutalist, capitalist office building.

The story begins with Jesse Faden, who is looking for her brother Dylan. That search leads her to enter the FBC (Federal Bureau of Control), a secret agency that works to hide from public view any kind of paranormal activity, with the help of her inner dialogue guiding the player through the world. These paranormal activities are called AWEs (Altered World Events), that can happen anywhere, alongside other concepts we will not mention to avoid spoilers. The game comes with its own terminology, which makes it more believable in the sense that this is a governmental agency that has everything categorized, defined, and buried in its own bureaucracy. And with bureaucracy, normally, comes hierarchy, which this agency also has. In the first minutes of the game, you find that the Director is dead (Trench), and by taking his weapon you become the new one, chosen by the Board. The Board is an unknown entity whose only shape and form we see is an inverted pyramid. And this is the setup, which, at least for me, is quite appealing.

But rapidly, my happiness and interest in the game start to fade as it progresses. Why, you ask?

**Gameplay**

The game is a third-person shooter with some extra powers that Jesse learns throughout the story by finding OoPs (Objects of Power). Although this would be a good opportunity to give her cool abilities that enhance combat, the majority of them are useless, not because they are bad, but because the enemy AI is bad. They are dumb as a rock. If you need to heal or reload, just step behind a pillar and they will shoot at it indefinitely. Even further, the gun and the telekinesis powers are the only ones you will ever need. Everything else is useless since the enemies are so basic and easily killed that you could just ram straight into them and probably not die — I did it a few times.

Even the other systems the game puts in place to make the gameplay more interesting are simply not appealing: the skill tree (I only upgraded telekinesis damage), the Board missions (I did almost none), the weapon and character mods (they do not matter at all). The side quests can be interesting for adding more to the story, but they require you to do more of the same gameplay that bores me so much. There are some puzzles in the game, but they are so few and so easy that we should not call them puzzles, let alone interesting.

**Story**

Like I said, the setting is pretty interesting and probably the strongest point of the game. But I do not know what the intention was in making everything so vague. It kind of matches the vibe of the setting, but at the same time the cutscenes reveal so little. Characters like Pope, Salvador, Arish and others have almost no development — they just tell you what you must do and that is it. Ahti is probably the most interesting character, but he speaks in riddles and does not have many meaningful moments where you can question whether he helps you out of goodness or something else entirely. I assume yes, but the game never commits to it.

In the end, you discover most of what you wanted to know, but if you want the full lore, you must hunt down all the collectibles and read and listen to all of them, which can be quite annoying.

**Verdict**

There are not many bugs, the story is interesting enough to keep you guessing and curious — although it is not well executed — and the gameplay is functional but boring and repetitive.
